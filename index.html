Literary Analysis 2

Archetypal Criticism The Crow and the Snake by Panchatantra

Summary

In this story, a pair of crows build a nest and lay eggs, but a snake living at the bottom of the tree keeps climbing up to eat them. No matter how hard the crows try to protect their eggs, the snake always returns. Feeling helpless, the crows ask a wise jackal for help. The jackal advises them to use a clever plan instead of fighting. The crow then steals a necklace from the queen’s palace and drops it into the snake’s hole. When the palace guards find the necklace there, they dig up the hole and kill the snake. After that, the crows can raise their young safely.

Analysis and Interpretation

Archetypal Elements in the Story

  • The Snake as the Villain - The snake represents danger, fear and destruction. Like many villains in stories, it threatens the lives of the innocent and creates constant harm.
  • The Crow as the Hero - Even though the crow is not powerful, it is determined to protect its family. This makes the crow a “hero” figure someone who refuses to give up despite suffering losses.
  • The Jackal as the Wise Helper - The jackal plays the role of the guide or mentor. He gives the crows the advice they need when they feel lost and hopeless.
  • Cleverness as the Winning Strategy - Instead of fighting strength with strength, the crows use a trick to defeat the snake. This is a common pattern in many stories where brains win over brute force.

Using the archetypal lens makes the story feel connected to many other tales across cultures. We recognize the snake, the hero and the helper because these character types appear in stories all over the world. Seeing these patterns helps us understand why the story is memorable: it teaches a simple but strong lesson about overcoming challenges using clever thinking rather than force.

Literary Analysis 3

Archetypal / Allegorical Lens Cupid and Psyche by Apuleius

Summary

Psyche is a princess so beautiful that people begin to praise her more than the goddess of beauty, stirring the goddess’ jealousy. Venus orders her son Cupid to make Psyche fall in love with a monster but Cupid instead falls deeply in love with her. Psyche is taken (by invisible forces) to a magical palace, where she lives with her mysterious husband whom she is forbidden to see. Though treated with kindness and luxury, Psyche becomes restless and persuaded by her jealous sisters secretly looks at him with a lamp, learning her husband is Cupid. Startled, she accidentally wounds him; he flees. Psyche is heartbroken and wanders the earth searching for him. To win him back, she must face a series of difficult tasks imposed by Venus. With help from nature and divine intervention, Psyche completes each task. In the end, Cupid brings Psyche before the gods and she is granted immortality they marry and Psyche becomes divine.

Analysis and Interpretation

Key archetypal and symbolic elements

  • Psyche as the Soul - The name “Psyche” literally means “soul.” Her journey from mortal perfection, to loss, to exile, to trials and finally divine union mirrors the soul’s path toward enlightenment or spiritual maturation
  • Cupid (Love) as Spiritual Guide - Cupid’s love for Psyche reflects “divine love” or a higher guiding force that draws the soul toward growth and immortality. Their forbidden marriage, hidden at first, represents the mysterious, inner connection between love and soul beyond physical appearance or societal expectations.
  • Trials as Initiation / Purification - The tasks imposed by Venus represent stages of purification, testing Psyche’s faith, patience, humility qualities often associated with spiritual or psychological growth.
  • Transition from Mortal to Divine - Psyche’s rise from vulnerable mortal to immortal goddess symbolizes transformation and transcendence a common motif in myths through suffering and endurance, the soul becomes something greater.

Cupid and Psyche as an allegory for the soul’s journey goes beyond its surface as a romantic myth. It becomes a story about human inner life, love, doubt, suffering, growth and eventual transcendence. It shows that love when rooted in trust and faith can lead to personal transformation. The trials represent inner psychological or spiritual tests rather than mere plot devices.

Literary Analysis 4

Marxist Criticism Kulakog and Tilmag a Philippine Folktale

Summary

The story of Kulakog and Tilmag tells about two creatures living in the same environment but having different strengths and behaviors. Tilmag is hardworking and resourceful, while Kulakog is lazy and relies on others. One day, their differences become clear: Tilmag works diligently to gather food and prepare for the future, while Kulakog takes advantage of her. In the end, Kulakog suffers the consequences of laziness, showing that effort and diligence are rewarded while idleness leads to failure.

Analysis and Interpretation

Key Archetypal and Symbolic Elements

  • Kulakog as the Hero / Laborer - Represents diligence, effort and personal responsibility. He is the archetypal “worker” who provides for himself and others.
  • Tilmag as the Trickster / Dependent - Symbolizes laziness and exploitation, taking advantage of others’ work instead of contributing.
  • Conflict Between Effort and Idleness - The struggle between the two characters symbolizes broader social tensions between productive and non-productive members of society.
  • Consequences as Moral Symbol - Tilmag’s suffering represents justice and the principle that one must work to reap rewards.

The story reflects real life social dynamics where hard work is sometimes taken for granted and inequality arises when some benefit without contributing. Through this lens, Kulakog and Tilmag is not only a moral tale about diligence but also a reflection on society, fairness and the dignity of labor.

Literary Analysis 6

Reader Response Criticism Morella by Edgar Allan Poe

Summary

Morella tells the story of a man who marries a highly intelligent and deeply philosophical woman named Morella. She studies metaphysics and explores the mysteries of life, death and identity. Morella becomes gravely ill and dies, but before her death, she expresses ideas about the immortality of the soul and reincarnation. After her death, the narrator and his new wife have a daughter. Shockingly, the child grows to resemble Morella in appearance and behavior. Eventually, the daughter herself dies and the narrator realizes that she is the reincarnation of Morella, highlighting the story’s themes of obsession, identity and the inescapable nature of the past.

Analysis and Interpretation

Key Archetypal and Symbolic Elements

  • Morella as the Mystic / Intellectual - Symbolizes forbidden knowledge, obsession and the mysteries of life and death.
  • The Daughter as Rebirth / Return - Represents reincarnation, fate and the cyclical nature of life.
  • The House and Setting - Gothic imagery of the home and tomb reflects the narrator’s inner fears and emotional turmoil.
  • Themes of Identity and Legacy - Both Morella and her daughter symbolize the idea that past influences and unresolved connections persist beyond death.

Using a Reader Response lens, the story emphasizes the interaction between the text and the reader’s emotions, imagination and personal interpretation. My main argument is that Morella creates a sense of suspense, horror and fascination by allowing readers to explore fear, grief and obsession through the narrator’s perspective.

Literary Analysis 5

Psychoanalytic Criticism A Rose for Emily by William Faulkner

Summary

A Rose for Emily tells the story of Emily Grierson, a reclusive Southern woman who lives in a decaying mansion in her hometown. After her father’s death, Emily isolates herself from society. She briefly becomes involved with a man named Homer Barron, but the relationship is shrouded in mystery. Emily refuses to acknowledge the passage of time, clinging to the past and resisting social change. After her death, townspeople discover Homer’s corpse in her house, revealing that Emily had been sleeping beside it for years. The story explores themes of isolation, obsession, decay and the effects of a controlling upbringing.

Analysis and Interpretation

Key Archetypal and Symbolic Elements

  • Emily as the Repressed Self - Represents repression, unresolved grief and fixation on the past.
  • The House - Symbolizes Emily’s mind and the stagnation of her life; its decay mirrors her psychological deterioration.
  • Homer Barron’s Corpse - Represents Emily’s inability to let go and the extremes of obsession and denial.
  • The Rose (the symbolic in the title) - Suggests secrecy, remembrance, or pity for Emily; an acknowledgment of her tragic inner life.

Using a Psychoanalytic lens, the story can be interpreted as an exploration of Emily’s unconscious mind, repression and unresolved conflicts. My main argument is that Emily’s behavior is driven by a combination of childhood trauma, fear of abandonment and an inability to reconcile reality with her desires.

Literary Analysis 1

Formalist Criticism Psalm 23 and Job 38, The Bible

Summary

Psalm 23 expresses trust in God as a shepherd who guides, protects, and provides for the believer. The psalmist describes walking “through the valley of the shadow of death” without fear, emphasizing divine care, guidance and comfort.

Job 38 is part of s God’s response to Job after his suffering. God questions Job, highlighting the vastness of creation, natural order and human limitations. Through rhetorical questions about the universe, weather and animals, God emphasizes divine power, wisdom and the limits of human understanding.

Analysis and Interpretation

Key Archetypal and Symbolic Elements

  • Shepherd (Psalm 23) - Symbolizes guidance, care and protection; a universal archetype of leadership and nurturing.
  • Valley of Shadows (Psalm 23) - Represents trials, death, or danger, the challenges of life.
  • God’s Questions (Job 38) - Symbolize the unknowable, divine wisdom and human limitation.
  • Nature Imagery (Job 38): Mountains, sea and weather highlight order, beauty and power, reflecting the complexity of the universe and the divine.

A formalist reading highlights how literary devices—structure, imagery, tone and metaphor—shape meaning. By focusing on the text itself rather than external context, we see how careful composition creates spiritual and emotional impact, making the messages of guidance, trust and humility universally powerful and enduring.

Summary of Learning

Collective Insights on Human Experience

Across the six texts I analyzed, one of the strongest insights about human experience is the constant tension between personal desires and the forces that shape our lives. Whether the stories focus on love, loss, identity or inner conflict, each narrative reveals how individuals struggle to understand themselves while navigating the world’s uncertainties. Many of the characters confront situations that challenge their values, push them toward transformation or force them to face uncomfortable truths about themselves. Themes such as resilience, powerlessness, moral choice and emotional vulnerability appear repeatedly, showing that human experience is rarely simple. Instead, it is shaped by layers of internal and external pressures that influence how we act, what we believe and who we become.

Society and Culture

Collectively, the texts also highlight how society and culture deeply influence human behavior. Through different settings and historical contexts, the stories reveal the power of social expectations, class structures, traditions and institutions in shaping people’s opportunities and limitations. Many of the works expose inequalities between genders, social classes or cultural groups showing how certain individuals benefit from privilege while others struggle against systemic barriers. At the same time, the texts critique the norms that societies often treat as natural or unquestionable. By presenting characters who challenge, conform to, or suffer under these norms, the stories offer insight into how culture both guides and restricts us. Together, these texts demonstrate that literature not only reflects the societies that produced it but also encourages readers to question and rethink the world they live in.

Major Themes and Patterns

Throughout the six stories, several themes and narrative patterns emerged that created strong connections between the texts. Many of the works explore the internal conflicts people experience when faced with difficult moral choices, while others reveal how deeply individuals are shaped by the societies they live in. Across the readings, I noticed recurring symbols and archetypes such as the journey, the outsider and moments of transformation that emphasize universal struggles shared across different times and cultures.

Theme 1 : The Struggle for Identity

A recurring theme across multiple texts is the characters’ search for identity. Whether they are questioning their role in society, struggling with inner conflicts or redefining their beliefs, many protagonists face moments where they must confront who they truly are. This theme appears in stories where characters resist external expectations, challenge traditional roles or try to understand their purpose. The texts collectively show that identity is not fixed; it is shaped by personal experiences, relationships and the pressures of the world around us.

Theme 2 : Power and Social Inequality

Another major theme is the presence of power dynamics and the social inequalities that influence human behavior. Several texts highlight how class, gender and authority structures determine people’s choices and limit their freedom. Characters often find themselves caught between those who hold power and those who are marginalized. These stories reveal how society creates systems that benefit a few while leaving others vulnerable and how individuals either conform to, resist or are broken by these systems.

Theme 3 : Transformation Through Conflict

Many of the stories also emphasize how conflict whether internal, interpersonal or societal drives transformation. Characters often face situations that force them to change, adapt or see the world differently. These conflicts lead to realizations about justice, responsibility, truth or human nature. Across the texts, transformation appears not as a single moment but as a gradual process shaped by struggle, reflection, and resilience.

Key Realizations

Studying these texts together helped me see how literature reveals patterns of human behavior that repeat across different periods and cultures. One of my most important realizations is that even though the stories come from diverse backgrounds, they address universal questions about identity, power and morality. I was surprised by how deeply societal structures influence personal choices and how characters often fight silent battles shaped by expectations they cannot escape. This challenged my earlier assumption that literature is mainly about individual stories, now I understand that each narrative also reflects bigger social forces. I also gained a new perspective on how conflict can act as a catalyst for growth, showing that transformation often comes from discomfort rather than certainty. Overall, reading these works together strengthened my awareness of the complexities of human experience and the many factors that shape who we become.

Final Reflection

Evolution of My Literary Understanding

Throughout this Great Books course, my way of reading stories has changed a lot. At first, I focused mostly on what happened in the story. Over time, I learned to look deeper, thinking about characters, symbols, themes and the lessons behind the story. The different critical lenses helped me see that every story has many layers and can be understood in several ways. I also learned to be an active reader — someone who asks questions, reflects and thinks about what the story means, instead of just reading for fun. I became better at noticing details, interpreting meaning and connecting stories to life, society and human nature.

The Critical Lenses: Challenges and Insights

Most Challenging Lens

The Psychoanalytic lens was the hardest for me. It required me to think about the characters’ unconscious feelings and motivations, like fears or desires they might not understand themselves. At first, it was confusing to connect these ideas to the story events. By rereading stories like A Rose for Emily and focusing on clear examples of obsession or fear, I learned how to apply this lens. It taught me to notice the inner struggles of characters and understand their actions more deeply.

Most Insightful Lens

The Reader-Response lens was the most useful for me. It showed that reading is not just about the story itself, but also about how I react to it. While reading Morella, my feelings of fear and curiosity shaped my understanding. This lens taught me to pay attention to my own reactions and realize that different readers may interpret the same story in different ways. I will continue using this approach in future reading.

Impact on Thinking, Values and Worldview

Reading these Great Books helped me think about life, society and human nature in new ways. Stories like Cupid and Psyche and The Crow and the Snake made me reflect on love, intelligence and morality. I also learned more about fairness, resilience and spiritual growth from texts like Psalm 23 and Job 38. These stories made me more thoughtful, empathetic and aware of human struggles. I now see literature as a tool to understand people, society and myself better.

This course was an important learning experience. It helped me think critically and reflect on stories in deeper ways. Using the lenses and reading different types of texts showed me that stories are more than entertainment — they teach lessons about life, human nature, and society. I will take these skills and insights with me, not just in school but in how I read, think and understand the world in the future.
